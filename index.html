49. Mendel and the peas: Gregor Mendel is an Austrian Monk who is considered the father of genetics. He studied the inheritance of various traits in pea plants. One such trait is whether the pea is smooth or wrinkled. Mendel predicted a ratio of 3 smooth peas for every 1 wrinkled pea. In one study, he randomly sampled some peas and found 423 smooth and 133 wrinkled peas. Do these data provide convincing evidence that his prediction is correct? (If I don't tell you the significance level, assume α = 5%)

1
State:
P = true proportion of peas that are wrinkled H0: p = 0.25  (25%) Ha: p ≠ 0.25 α = 5% = 0.05
2
Plan: If conditions are met, we will do a 1-Proportion Z-Test.
Random?: Sampling is done. ✓
Independence?: 556 peas is most likely less than 10% of all peas that he would check at the time. ✓
Large Counts?: (556)(0.25) = 139 ≥ 10  and  (556)(0.75) = 417 ≥ 10 ✓
Can use Normal approximation.
3
Do:
p̂ = 133556 = 0.2392 ≈ 23.92% σp̂ = (0.25)(0.75)556 = 0.0184 z = p̂ − p0σp̂ = 0.2392 − 0.250.0184 = −0.587
normalcdf(−∞, −0.587, 0, 1) = 0.2786 P-value = 2 × 0.2786 = 0.5572 ≈ 55.72%
4
Conclude:
Since our p-value of 55.72% is greater than our α of 5%, we fail to reject the null hypothesis given in 1. There is not sufficient evidence that the proportion of peas that are wrinkled is not 25%. (i.e., Mendel's prediction appears to be correct.)

50. DMV Driving Test: A state's Division of Motor Vehicles claims that 60% of all teens pass their driving test on the first attempt. An investigative reporter examines an SRS of the DMV records for 125 teens; 86 of them passed the test on their first try. Is there convincing evidence that the DMV's claim is incorrect?

1
State:
P = true proportion of teens who would pass their driving test on their first attempt H0: p = 0.60  (60%) Ha: p ≠ 0.60 α = 5% = 0.05
2
Plan: If conditions are met, we will do a 1-Proportion Z-Test.
Random?: SRS was done. ✓
Independence?: 125 is most likely less than 10% of all teens who take the driving test. ✓
Large Counts?: (0.6)(125) = 75 ≥ 10  and  (0.4)(125) = 50 ≥ 10 ✓
Can use Normal approximation.
3
Do:
p̂ = 86125 = 0.688 = 68.8% σp̂ = (0.6)(0.4)125 = 0.0438 ≈ 4.4% z = p̂ − p0σp̂ = 0.688 − 0.600.0438 ≈ 2.01
normalcdf(2.01, ∞, 0, 1) = 0.0227 P-value = 2 × 0.0227 = 0.0454 ≈ 4.54%
4
Conclude:
Since our p-value of 4.54% is less than our α of 5%, we reject the null hypothesis given in 1. There is convincing evidence that the true proportion of teens who would pass their driving test on their first attempt is not 60%. (i.e., The DMV's claim appears to be incorrect.)
